The other day, I was talking to the owner of an Italian restaurant the other day.

We were giving her restaurant a digital makeover, including:

  • Recreating the menu

  • Designing the website

  • Dreaming up chatbots that could act like servers taking your drink order

  • Using AR codes to place augmented reality pizzas on the table so that people could see the food they were thinking about ordering

(Note to self: a chatbot is a better mechanism at conveying information about a company than a website is)

Anyhoo, when we started talking strategy after the boring stuff was out of the way, I told her that our software developers don’t speak in language anymore. That made her stop. She asked me what I meant, and I sent her a map of Netflix created by Simon Wardley, the reason Ubuntu Linux is the most popular flavour of Linux for Cloud Computing.

Language is rich. It can be used to describe anything. The problem is, the same words mean vastly different things to different people.

Therefore, if you want to be clear in your communication, you have to leverage the human senses.

  • Kinesthetic aka Touch

  • Auditory aka Hearing

  • Read/Write aka Reading and Writing

  • Visual aka Seeing

  • Olfactory aka Smelling

One of the most overlooked ways to increase the clarity of a communication is by adding visuals to text.
