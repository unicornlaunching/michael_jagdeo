Wardley Mapping For Account Executives in High-Ticket Sales

You’re an Account Executive. Your responsibility is to make sales worth hundreds if not millions of dollars per transaction.

So…what’s your sales strategy?

Stop reading here and say your sales strategy out loud.

…

Here - I’ll go first because clearly you’re nervous.

My sales strategy believes that during Show & Tell, it’s better to spend 80% of your time showing and 20% of your time telling. Therefore, my sales strategy is to create and pitch prototypes to key targets across industries such that we can evaluate businesses worth building before wasting time on venture capital and debt financing to conquer a problem that isn’t even worth solving in the first place.

But you probably don’t have the freedom that I do. Whatever new sales strategy you decide to adopt, it will have to align with the key artificial metrics created by Sales Executives in Sales Organizations.

The artificial metrics created by bosses in sales include:

  • Number of phone calls made

  • Number of leads generated

  • Number of demos booked

  • Number of demos completed

  • Number of proposals sent

It’s not likely that you have the organizational power to change these metrics…so any strategy you implement will have to fit within those metric targets.

Sniper-Driven Development

I’m going to make one more assumption here. I’m going to assume that you’re engaging in Account-Based Marketing. Account-Based Marketing is where your Sales Team decides which accounts they want to close AND THEN DOES EVERYTHING IT TAKES TO CLOSE THOSE SPECIFIC ACCOUNTS.

In Account-Based Marketing, you may be given targets like Microsoft, Google, Apple, Netflix, etc. These are tough targets that can take years to close and win Vendor Of Record status.

But, if your company’s purpose involves selling to these multinational corporations…your sales strategy is going to have to close at least one of these accounts.

So…what do you do? What’s your sales strategy for closing a high-ticket sale with a Fortune 500?

OK. Enough preamble. Let’s now focus on how you can use Wardley Mapping and The Military Decision-Making Process along with OCOKA and John Boyd’s Patterns of Conflict to create a sales strategy uniquely tailored towards the realities of 2023.

As-Is vs. To-Be

Every sales strategy starts out with:

  1. Who are we? Who are we in comparison to the market leader?

  2. Who do we want to sell? How are we going to meet them? Where do they congregate? How might we close a sale with them? How long does it take the average sale to close from lead identification to sale closed?

Examples:

  • We’re a cybersecurity company who’s an underdog that wants to sell our software solution to hospitals and we’re going to sell them by booking software demos with key decision makers across specific hospitals in Toronto with at least 1000 beds. It takes us 18 months to sell a hospital.

  • We’re a data analytics company with a Shopify Plugin who’s an underdog that wants to sell our software solution to the top sellers on Shopify with at least $1M in annual revenue. It takes us three months to close new leads.

But how do you create a sales strategy now that you know who you want to sell? There are a lot of frameworks out there. Sales Funnels, Click Funnels, Value Ladders, etc. Here’s an innovative approach I came across that resonates with me.

More Than Words

The problem with sales strategies is that they’re full of words.

Words are just one way to express ourselves. If we want to fully express ourselves, we must leverage to four modalities of communication called KARWV:

  • Kinesthetic - we learn by touching and doing

  • Auditory - we learn by hearing and listening

  • Read/Write - we learn by reading and writing

  • Visual - we learn by seeing and observing

If we want to articulate a sales strategy clearly, we must therefore leverage all of these modalities. We must express ourselves in a way that incorporates all of these preferred learning styles.
